# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Primary: Potential donors evaluating whether to support the yeshiva. They arrive via word of mouth, referrals from existing donors, or community networks. They are typically Orthodox Jewish adults, often American or Israeli, comfortable in both Hebrew and English. They are looking to understand who this institution is, feel a connection to it, and be moved to give.

Secondary: Families of current students (checking in, seeing photos, staying proud), alumni (staying connected), prospective students.

The primary action on every screen is emotional, not transactional: "Do I feel something for this place?"

## Product Purpose

An institutional website for Yeshivat Pe'er Yisroel, a Torah institution named after the holy Rebbe R' Yisrael of Stolin ZT"L. The site exists to represent the yeshiva's identity to the outside world, showcase its life and spirit, and create a bridge between the institution and potential supporters. Success means a donor arrives as a stranger and leaves feeling like family.

## Brand Personality

Holy, traditional, warm. The yeshiva carries centuries of Torah heritage; the site should feel like it belongs to that lineage. Not a startup, not a charity, not a movement — a place of learning with roots, warmth, and real human presence.

## Anti-references

- Christian-church-aesthetic sites: soft American gradients, sans-serif inspirational quotes, stock photos of sunsets and hands.
- Israeli government / bureaucratic sites: dense text, institutional gray, no visual soul.
- Generic charity/fundraising platforms: urgent red banners, progress bars, corporate blue buttons.

## Design Principles

1. **Warmth earns trust before the ask.** A donor must feel close to the place before being invited to give. Every design choice — photo selection, type, color — should build intimacy, not formality.
2. **Tradition is the aesthetic.** The visual language should feel continuous with the culture it represents. Rich color, considered typography, and real photography carry more credibility than trend-following.
3. **Restraint in chrome, generosity in content.** The interface should be quiet so the photos and stories can be loud. Remove decorative noise; serve the content.
4. **Hebrew-first, English-equal.** The site is bilingual by nature. RTL/LTR switching is a feature, not a footnote. Neither language should feel secondary.
5. **A visitor is a guest, not a user.** The tone is welcoming and unhurried. No dark patterns, no urgency theater, no conversion-rate thinking that shows.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AAA compliance. Full keyboard navigation, high-contrast text, screen reader support, and reduced-motion respect for all animations.
